\begin{abstract}
	Robotic manipulators in safety-critical deployments are susceptible to joint failures that
	cause abrupt performance degradation, yet classical fault-tolerant control relies on analytical
	models of the post-fault dynamics and hand-engineered recovery laws that do not generalise
	across the fault-mode distribution. We present \textbf{COMPENSATE}, a teacher-student
	reinforcement learning framework for fault-tolerant manipulation comprising three components:
	a \emph{privileged teacher ensemble} of mode-specialised PPO policies, one per failure
	configuration; a \emph{fault-conditioned student} that consumes a one-hot fault indicator
	concatenated with the proprioceptive observation and is supervised by behavioural cloning under
	hard per-environment teacher routing; and a \emph{staged fault curriculum} during teacher
	optimisation, coupled with zero-shot cross-simulator evaluation in mjlab that rejects policies
	exploiting simulator-specific contact integration prior to hardware deployment. On a Franka
	Research~3 reach task under per-joint permanent lockouts, the privileged teachers attain
	$89.4\%$ mean success at a $0.038$~m mean end-effector position error on the seven
	single-joint lockouts, and the fault-conditioned
	student retains $65.0\%$ success at $0.051$~m, exceeding a diffusion policy distilled from the
	same supervision and fault conditioning by $16$ percentage points of success while halving the
	residual position error. The $\approx\!24$-point teacher-to-student deficit quantifies the
	behavioural-cloning loss incurred when the per-mode specialist ensemble is compressed into a
	single fault-conditioned network and substantiates the architectural decomposition that
	separates mode-specific policy optimisation from unified, conditioning-driven deployment.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

	Robotic manipulators are a cornerstone of modern industrial applications, performing repetitive,
	high-precision tasks with speed and reliability~\citep{ali2018vision, ghodsian2023mobile}. Recent
	advances in artificial intelligence~(AI) have expanded the capabilities of robotic systems, enabling
	them to learn, adapt, and make independent decisions~\citep{sun2021artificial, liu2021deep,
	li2024manipllm}.  Despite these advances, a fundamental vulnerability persists: mechanical
	malfunctions (arising from joint wear, actuator fatigue, or unexpected environmental loading) can
	abruptly degrade performance or cause complete task failure~\citep{291826, sciavicco2012modelling}.
	As manipulators are deployed in safety-critical domains such as healthcare, manufacturing, and field
	robotics, the ability to maintain task performance through joint failures has become essential, while
	most deployed systems still rely on predefined, fault-unaware control
	policies~\citep{deloitte2025smart, allaboutai2025report}.

	Traditional fault management strategies address this vulnerability through hardware redundancy and
	fault diagnosis and isolation~(FDI). Redundancy-based approaches add actuators or parallel kinematic
	chains so that a single joint failure does not halt operation~\citep{291826, anand2012fault,
	mokhtari2022fault}, but incur substantial cost, weight, and design complexity. FDI techniques
	(spanning signal-processing anomaly detection~\citep{kimevaluation, ompusunggu2014winding}, rule-based
	expert systems~\citep{li2023open}, and model-based deviation monitors~\citep{shuai2022research,
	sabry2019fault}) can identify that a fault has occurred, yet they do not synthesize an adapted control
	policy in response; a separate, hand-crafted recovery layer is still required.

	Machine learning has begun to close this gap. Predictive maintenance and anomaly detection models can
	anticipate failures before they occur or flag deviations during operation~\citep{lian2022anomaly,
	eski2011fault}.  Recent work has demonstrated learning directly from observed robot failures
	(including recovery in long-horizon manipulation tasks)~\citep{huang2025fail2progress}.
	Generative approaches such as counterfactual failure synthesis can further produce diverse fault
	rollouts from successful demonstrations, reaching 81.3\% correction accuracy~\citep{wang2025learning}.
	Nevertheless, these methods presuppose either a corpus of failure demonstrations or a
	hand-crafted recovery policy downstream of detection. Even when a fault has been correctly
	identified, the question of \emph{what to do next} is left to a separate model-based controller
	whose recovery behaviour does not scale across the diversity of possible faults.

	The robotics community has increasingly turned to reinforcement learning~(RL) to overcome this
	limitation. For legged locomotion, contrastive forward-prediction RL enables agents to detect joint
	damage from proprioceptive signals alone and immediately reconfigure gait without any explicit fault
	label~\citep{fu2025contrastive}. For visuomotor policies, latent-space RL can steer a pretrained
	diffusion policy toward successful outcomes with minimal additional
	samples~\citep{wagenmaker2025steering}, and sim-to-real transfer via massive parallel sampling with
	active domain exploration has demonstrated 42\%-63\% performance gains over strong
	baselines~\citep{sobanbabu2025sampling}. These results confirm that RL-based adaptation is a
	principled direction for fault-tolerant robotics, yet they only address locomotion. The specific
	challenge of reactive joint-failure compensation for \emph{robot manipulator} reaching tasks in real
	hardware deployments remains underexplored.
    \begin{figure}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{figures/COMPENSATE.pdf}
        \caption{Demonstrates the workflow of COMPENSATE framework for creating a fault tolerant policy.}
        \label{fig:COMPENSATE}
    \end{figure}
	To address this gap, we present \textbf{COMPENSATE}, a teacher-student RL
	framework for fault-tolerant manipulator control. The framework is composed of three
	interdependent components:
	\emph{(i)}~A \emph{privileged teacher ensemble} in which one proximal policy optimization (PPO) specialist is trained for each failure mode, such that mode-specific compensations are not averaged away by a single multi-task
	agent. \emph{(ii)}~A \emph{fault-aware student} distilled by hard per-environment teacher
	routing: the student receives an explicit fault indicator alongside the proprioceptive
	observation and is supervised at each environment by the matching specialist, so that a single
	deployable network carries the full per-mode behaviour repertoire of the ensemble. \emph{(iii)}~A \emph{staged
	fault curriculum} for the teachers, paired with cross-simulator validation in mjlab that acts as
	an inexpensive sim-to-real probe filtering out engine-specific artifacts before any physical
	trial. We instantiate the framework on a Franka Research~3 model and report a preliminary
	evaluation on the Reach task under per-joint permanent lockouts; remaining failure regimes,
	tasks, and hardware deployments are noted as ongoing work.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

	\textbf{Classical fault-tolerant control.} Fault-tolerant control for robotic manipulators has long
	relied on model-based methods. Early strategies applied passive and active hardware redundancy so that
	joint failures would not halt operation, while fault diagnosis and isolation~(FDI) detected deviations
	from known system models~\citep{blanke, 291826, sciavicco2012modelling}. More recent designs embed 	backstepping or sliding-mode controllers that maintain tracking under actuator
	faults~\citep{anand2012fault, mokhtari2022fault, act11120353}. These methods are analytically rigorous
	but require accurate dynamic models and hand-crafted recovery rules, limiting their applicability to
	anticipated, well-characterized fault modes.

	\textbf{Learning-based fault detection and diagnosis.} Data-driven methods have substantially improved
	fault detection speed and accuracy. Neural networks, deep belief networks, and power-signature analysis
	can flag incipient anomalies before hard failures occur~\citep{eski2011fault, sabry2019fault,
	shuai2022research, raouf2022mechanical}. Inverse reinforcement learning has been applied to
	simultaneously detect anomalies and propose corrective actions in autonomous
	systems~\citep{lian2022anomaly}, and deep-learning diagnosis has been extended to multi-fault
	industrial robots~\citep{adam2023multiple}. Detection alone, however, does not yield an adapted
	control policy; a separate recovery layer is still required, and these methods are unreliable when
	failures evolve gradually with no labeled fault data available.

	\textbf{Reinforcement learning for fault-tolerant robots.} RL has emerged as a compelling framework
	for learning adaptive behaviors without explicit failure models. In legged locomotion, contrastive
	forward-prediction RL enables quadrupeds to detect joint damage from proprioceptive signals alone and
	immediately reconfigure gait without any fault label~\citep{fu2025contrastive}. Learning from observed
	failure trajectories has also shown promise for manipulation recovery~\citep{huang2025fail2progress,
	wang2025learning}, and RL has been compared favorably against model predictive control for
	fault-tolerant continuous control~\citep{ahmed2018comparison}. For manipulators specifically,
	\citet{pham2024adaptive} formulated joint-failure compensation as a partially observable Markov
	decision process and demonstrated high task success, but addressed only single-joint, simulation-only
	scenarios, leaving multi-fault and real-hardware settings open.

	\textbf{Teacher-student distillation and sim-to-real transfer.} Teacher-student RL has become the
	standard paradigm for deploying policies whose training-time supervision is richer than the
	signal available at test time. A privileged teacher trained with full state access (e.g., exact
	terrain or contact properties) is distilled into a student that operates on deployable
	observations alone~\citep{kumar2021rma}. Latent-space RL can further steer a pre-trained policy
	toward correct behavior with minimal additional samples~\citep{wagenmaker2025steering}, and
	active-exploration system identification during sim-to-real transfer yields 42\%-63\%
	performance gains over standard domain randomization~\citep{sobanbabu2025sampling}.
	COMPENSATE adapts this paradigm to manipulator fault tolerance with a different decomposition:
	rather than distilling one privileged teacher into a partially-observed student, we train an
	\emph{ensemble} of per-mode PPO specialists and distill them into a single fault-conditioned
	student, so the privilege removed at deployment is the redundancy of running multiple per-mode
	networks, not knowledge of the fault itself. The next section formalises this setting as a
	Partially Observable Markov Decision Process~(POMDP) and develops the three components in turn.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

	A single PPO agent confronted with the full distribution of fault modes receives gradient
	signals from qualitatively different optimal compensations and converges to an averaged,
	often infeasible, behaviour. \textbf{COMPENSATE} avoids this multi-task interference by
	decomposing learning into two stages: per-mode PPO specialists are trained in isolation, then
	their behaviours are compressed into a single fault-conditioned deployable network. Three
	components implement this split: a \emph{privileged teacher ensemble}
	(\S\ref{subsec:teachers}) that converges to mode-specific compensations, a \emph{staged fault
	curriculum} (\S\ref{subsec:curriculum}) that keeps each PPO specialist stable as its active
	fault set widens, and a \emph{fault-aware student} (\S\ref{subsec:distillation}) that receives
	an explicit fault indicator at deployment and is supervised by hard per-environment teacher
	routing. We first formalise the setting.

	\subsection{Problem Formulation}
	\label{subsec:problem}

		We model joint-failure-resilient manipulation as a POMDP
		$\langle\mathcal{S},\mathcal{O},\mathcal{A},T,R,\gamma\rangle$. The full state
		$s\in\mathcal{S}$ contains the joint positions $q\in \mathbb{R}^n$, joint velocities $\dot q$, and the
		currently active failure configuration. The student's observation
		$o\in\mathcal{O}$ comprises relative joint positions, relative joint velocities, the previous
		action $a_{t-1}$, the goal end-effector pose $g=(p_g,\mathbf{q}_g)$ with position
		$p_g\in\mathbb{R}^{3}$ and unit quaternion $\mathbf{q}_g\in\mathbb{S}^{3}$, and an
		explicit fault indicator $f\in\mathcal{F}$ encoded as a one-hot vector over the failure
		set. The teachers see the same observation but their fault indicator is, by construction,
		constant within each training run (each teacher is trained against a single mode). The
		shared fault-aware observation is what allows the per-mode specialists to be compressed
		into a single deployable network at distillation time.

		Actions $a\in\mathcal{A}\subset \mathbb{R}^n$ are relative joint-position increments scaled by
		$\alpha\in \mathbb{R}_{>0}$. We consider a set $\mathcal{F}=\{f_1 \ldots f_n\}$ of failure configurations comprising
		single-joint permanent lockouts and a healthy baseline; degraded-stiffness and
		multi-joint variants are introduced in~\S\ref{subsec:curriculum}. Under lockout
		$f_i$, joint $i$ holds its last commanded position regardless of the network
		output, modeling a fault that the controller cannot bypass at the actuator
		level:
		\begin{equation}
		    q_t^{\mathrm{cmd}, i} =
		        \begin{cases}
		            q_{t-1}^{\mathrm{cmd}, i} + \alpha\,a_t^i & \text{joint } i \text{ healthy,}\\
		            q_{t-1}^{\mathrm{cmd}, i}                  & \text{joint } i \text{ locked.}
		        \end{cases}
		    \label{eq:action}
		\end{equation}
		Modeling the fault inside the action transition (rather than as an external
		disturbance on $T$) ensures that the gradient seen by the teacher reflects what
		the deployed arm will actually execute, eliminating a source of train--test
		drift that would otherwise have to be handled by domain randomization.

	\subsection{Privileged Teacher Ensemble}
	\label{subsec:teachers}

		Different joint failures require qualitatively different optimal compensations; e.g.,
		a locked shoulder must be circumvented by elbow and wrist redundancy, while a
		locked wrist requires a base-driven reorientation. A single PPO agent confronted
		with the full fault distribution receives gradient signals that pull these
		solutions toward an averaged, often infeasible, behavior. We therefore train
		one specialist~$\pi^c$ per configuration $c\in\mathcal{F}$ using
		PPO~\citep{schulman2017proximal}, treating each teacher as a privileged oracle
		that has implicit knowledge of its own fault through the environment it was
		exposed to during training.

		Each teacher optimises a weighted sum of an end-effector tracking objective and motion
		regularisers. Let $p_\mathrm{ee}\in\mathbb{R}^{3}$ and $\mathbf{q}_\mathrm{ee}\in\mathbb{S}^{3}$
		denote the current end-effector position and unit-quaternion orientation, and let $p_g$,
		$\mathbf{q}_g$ be the goal pose components from \S\ref{subsec:problem}. With
		$a_t\in\mathbb{R}^{7}$ the commanded action and $\dot q_t\in\mathbb{R}^{9}$ the measured
		joint velocity, the teacher reward at step $t$ is
		\begin{equation}
		    \begin{aligned}
		        r_t = \;&w_\mathrm{pos}\,\|p_\mathrm{ee}-p_g\|_2
		            + w_\mathrm{fine}\bigl(1-\tanh(\|p_\mathrm{ee}-p_g\|_2/\sigma)\bigr) \\
		            &+ w_\mathrm{ori}\cdot 2\arccos\!\bigl(|\langle\mathbf{q}_\mathrm{ee},\mathbf{q}_g\rangle|\bigr)
		            + w_\mathrm{ar}\,\|a_t-a_{t-1}\|_2^{2}
		            + w_\mathrm{jv}\,\|\dot q_t\|_2^{2},
		    \end{aligned}
		    \label{eq:reward}
		\end{equation}
		where the five terms are, in order, an $L_2$ coarse-position penalty (m), a tanh-kernel
		fine-positioning term with bandwidth $\sigma=0.1$~m, the geodesic orientation error in
		radians, an $L_2$ action-rate penalty, and an $L_2$ joint-velocity penalty. The weights
		$\{w_\mathrm{pos}, w_\mathrm{fine}, w_\mathrm{ar}, w_\mathrm{jv}\}$ follow the linear
		curriculum specified in \S\ref{subsec:impl} (sign-flipping $w_\mathrm{fine}$ from an early
		penalty into a late-training bonus), while $w_\mathrm{ori}$ is held constant. The schedule
		enforces that the agent first learns to \emph{reach} under the active fault before being
		asked to do so \emph{smoothly}.
		Without this annealing, we observed teachers collapsing to a still posture that
		trivially satisfies the regulariser. Goal poses are sampled by drawing random
		joint configurations, computing forward kinematics, and rejecting any pose
		outside the reachable workspace of the locked joint, so the reward signal is
		never dominated by physically unreachable targets, an effect that would
		otherwise bias the teacher toward unreachable-target avoidance rather than
		fault compensation.

	\subsection{Fault-Mode Curriculum}
	\label{subsec:curriculum}

		Even with one specialist per fault, exposing a teacher to all variants of its
		target mode from the first step (e.g., lockouts at different commanded
		positions, or jointly with stiffness degradation) destabilizes early training:
		the agent cannot disambiguate whether a failed reach is due to its own policy
		or to a fault it has not yet learned to model. We therefore use an incremental
		curriculum that begins with the teacher's target failure in isolation and
		expands the active fault set $\mathcal{C}_k\subseteq\mathcal{F}$ in stages over
		the course of training. This decouples skill acquisition from fault inference
		and is the only mechanism, aside from the privileged-teacher decomposition
		itself, that we found necessary to obtain stable PPO convergence under
		multi-joint and degraded-stiffness faults. The student does not use a
		curriculum.  By the time distillation begins, all teachers are fixed, so
		uniform sampling over $\mathcal{F}$ poses no instability.

	\subsection{Fault-Aware Student Distillation}
	\label{subsec:distillation}

		The student~$\pi^S$ is the policy that will actually be deployed. It receives the same
		observation as the teachers, including the one-hot fault indicator
		$f\in\mathcal{F}$, so its job is not to detect the active fault but to compress the
		per-mode specialists $\{\pi^c\}_{c\in\mathcal{F}}$ into a single conditional network
		$\pi^S(\cdot\mid o, f)$. We adopt this design rather than the alternative of inferring
		the fault from kinematic history because (i) collapsing the ensemble into one
		fault-conditioned network removes the per-mode selection step that would otherwise be
		required at deployment, and (ii) inference quality and control quality decouple, so the
		student's residual error is attributable to behaviour cloning and capacity, not to latent
		fault disambiguation. An upstream fault-detection module is assumed to supply $f$ at
		deployment time; replacing that module with a learned detector is left to future work.

		At each distillation reset, every parallel environment is independently assigned a
		configuration $c$ and routed to the corresponding teacher~$\pi^c$. That teacher provides
		the behavioural target for that environment for the entire episode, and the student is
		conditioned on $f = \text{one\_hot}(c)$. We use this \emph{hard routing} rather than
		averaging teacher logits because the teachers represent mode-specific compensations that
		are non-interpolable, so averaging would supervise the student with actions no single
		teacher would take. Given minibatches $\{(o_k, f_k, a_k^c)\}_{k=1}^{B}$, the student
		minimizes the per-action behavioural-cloning loss
		\begin{equation}
		    \mathcal{L}_\mathrm{BC} =
		        \frac{1}{B}\sum_{k=1}^{B}
		        \bigl\|\pi^S(o_k, f_k) - a_k^c\bigr\|_2^{2}.
		    \label{eq:bc}
		\end{equation}
		Convergence of~$\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{BC}$ certifies that the per-mode behaviours of the
		ensemble fit into a single network indexed by $f$; the remaining gap to the per-mode PPO
		performance bound is then a direct measure of distillation loss. We now describe how
		these components are instantiated and evaluated.

\section{Experiments}
\subsection{Implementation Details}
\label{subsec:impl}

		All policies are trained in \textbf{IsaacLab} on a Franka Research~3 (FR3)
		model, then evaluated through a deliberate three-stage pipeline (IsaacLab,
		mjlab, and physical hardware), each stage acting as a stricter filter on the
		preceding one. The policy observation is 40-dimensional: relative joint
		positions $q\in\mathbb{R}^{9}$, relative joint velocities
		$\dot q\in\mathbb{R}^{9}$, the previous action $a_{t-1}\in\mathbb{R}^{7}$,
		a goal end-effector pose $g\in\mathbb{R}^{3}\times\mathbb{S}^{3}$, and a one-hot fault indicator
		$f\in\{0,1\}^{8}$ identifying the active configuration in $\mathcal{F}$.
		Actions are relative
		joint-position increments with scale $\alpha=0.03$~rad. The teacher set
		$\mathcal{F}$ contains $|\mathcal{F}|=8$ configurations: seven single-joint
		permanent lockouts (one per actuated FR3 joint) and a healthy baseline.
		The reach task resamples random 	end-effector goals every 6~s with success defined as $\le 0.04$~m position
		error and $\le 0.2$~rad orientation error.  Additive uniform observation noise
		of $\pm 0.01$~rad is injected on joint positions, velocities, and action
		feedback throughout training.

		\textbf{Networks and optimization.} Each teacher~$\pi^c$ is a two-layer MLP
		with hidden dimensions $[128,\,64]$ and ELU activations, trained with PPO for
		$3{,}500$ iterations using 24 rollout steps per parallel environment, an
		adaptive learning rate (target KL $=0.01$, initial $\eta=10^{-4}$), clip ratio
		$\epsilon=0.2$, and entropy coefficient $\beta=0.001$. Reward weights follow a linear
		curriculum over the first $5{,}000$ environment steps: $w_\mathrm{pos}$ is ramped from
		$-2.0$ to $-1.0$, $w_\mathrm{fine}$ from $-0.05$ to $+1.0$ (sign-flipping the tanh-kernel
		term from an early-training penalty into a late-training bonus once the agent reaches the
		coarse goal), $w_\mathrm{ar}$ from $0$ to $-0.01$, and $w_\mathrm{jv}$ from $0$ to
		$-0.001$, while $w_\mathrm{ori}$ is held at $-1.0$ throughout. The fault-mode curriculum
		expands $\mathcal{C}_k$ by one additional mode at each evenly-spaced fraction
		$i/|\mathcal{F}|$ of the total training step budget ($i=1,\ldots,|\mathcal{F}|$),
		implemented as a counter on \texttt{env.common\_step\_counter}. The student is a wider
		MLP $[128,\,128]$ with ELU activations, trained on the BC loss in
		Eq.~\ref{eq:bc} using Adam ($\eta=5\times 10^{-4}$), gradient accumulation
		over 15 steps, gradient clipping at norm~1.0, and 8 learning epochs per
		rollout.

		\textbf{Cross-simulator validation as a sim-to-real probe.} Before any
		hardware trial, the IsaacLab-trained student is evaluated zero-shot in
		\textbf{mjlab}~(MuJoCo~Warp) under a matched observation and action
		specification at a matched 30~Hz control rate (240~Hz physics, decimation~8).
		This step is methodological rather than logistical: it asks whether the
		learned compensation is a property of the manipulator dynamics or of the
		Isaac~Sim integrator. A policy that exploits engine-specific artifacts will
		pass IsaacLab evaluation but fail in mjlab, and would do the same on real
		hardware; mjlab thus serves as an inexpensive filter that catches this
		failure mode before risking a physical arm. Policies that transfer cleanly to
		mjlab are then deployed zero-shot on two physical manipulators, the Franka
		Research~3 and the Franka Emika Panda, completing the simulation-to-hardware
		pipeline.

\subsection{Tasks and Evaluation Protocol}

The COMPENSATE evaluation protocol covers two manipulation tasks (Reach and Lift), two classes of simulated joint fault (permanent lockout and degraded stiffness via artificially increased joint stiffness), and a sim-to-hardware pipeline that culminates in zero-shot deployment on the FR3 and Panda arms. Reach is the precise target-acquisition task and is the focus of the present manuscript; Lift extends the protocol to object lifting and is evaluated in the same way once the corresponding teacher specialists have been trained. The primary metric is task completion rate, with completion time reported as a secondary metric. Permanent lockouts constrain the affected joint to its last commanded position with zero velocity; degraded-stiffness faults preserve partial operability but inject diminished accuracy and compliance. Reported results below cover the Reach task under per-joint permanent lockouts; Lift results are reported in the appendix.

\subsection{Baselines}

To benchmark the proposed method, we compare it against two baselines: a standard PPO policy trained without joint failures, and a diffusion policy. The diffusion baseline is trained by rolling out the teacher specialists across all modelled joint-failure scenarios, aggregating the resulting transitions into a single dataset, and distilling that dataset into a diffusion policy conditioned on the same observation as COMPENSATE, including the fault indicator; this isolates the effect of generative-aggregation distillation versus the per-environment discriminative routing used by COMPENSATE.

\section{Experimental Results}
\label{sec:result}

	On the Franka Research~3 Reach task under per-joint permanent lockouts, the privileged
	specialist teachers attain a lockout-mean success rate of \textbf{89.4\%} at \textbf{0.038~m} mean
	end-effector error (with the healthy-baseline specialist at 94\%/0.037~m reported separately), and the fault-aware COMPENSATE student retains \textbf{65.0\%} success at
	\textbf{0.051~m}, outperforming a diffusion policy trained on the same teacher-generated data
	by \textbf{16 percentage points} of success and approximately halving its residual position
	error. The remainder of this section unpacks this headline through three questions, all
	evaluated on the \textbf{Reach} task. \emph{(Q1)} Can a specialist teacher recover end-effector
	reaching under a permanent single-joint lockout, and how does difficulty vary
	with the locked joint? \emph{(Q2)} Can a single fault-conditioned student
	reproduce the per-mode teacher behaviours from one network when supervised by
	hard per-environment routing?
	\emph{(Q3)} How does the student compare to a generative learning baseline
	trained on the same teacher data? Unless stated otherwise, all numbers are
	computed from 50 Reach episodes per (method, condition) cell using the
	success criterion of \S\ref{subsec:problem} ($\le 0.04$~m position error
	within the 6~s episode budget); we also report the minimum end-effector
	position error attained within each episode (mean, standard deviation, and
	best across the 50 trials) as a continuous proxy for compensation quality.
	Results on the Lift task, on degraded-stiffness and
	multi-joint faults, and on the PPO-without-failures baseline, follow
	the same protocol and are reported in the appendix and in the
	hardware-deployment section.

	\subsection{Per-Joint Teacher Performance}
	\label{subsec:results-teacher}

		Table~\ref{tab:teacher-per-joint} reports each specialist teacher evaluated
		under its own target lockout. Across the seven single-joint lockout configurations the
		teachers reach a mean success rate of \textbf{89.4\%} and a mean minimum
		position error of \textbf{0.038~m}, indicating that even with one full
		degree of freedom frozen the residual six-DoF chain is consistently driven
		to within the success tolerance of the unimpaired goal. The healthy-baseline
		specialist (column ``7 (healthy)'' in Table~\ref{tab:teacher-per-joint}) attains
		94\% success at 0.037~m, providing a non-fault ceiling against which the lockout
		means can be read. The two strongest
		conditions are joints~0 and~4 (both 100\% success), which lie at the

	\subsection{Distilled Student vs. Diffusion Baseline}
	\label{subsec:results-student}

		Table~\ref{tab:agg} compares the fault-aware COMPENSATE student against the
		diffusion baseline aggregated across the full joint-failure distribution.
		We report two independent training seeds per method to expose run-to-run
		variability. Averaged over seeds, COMPENSATE attains \textbf{65.0\%} success
		(56\%, 74\%) at a mean minimum error of \textbf{0.051~m}, while the
		diffusion baseline reaches \textbf{49.0\%} (32\%, 66\%) at \textbf{0.100~m}.
		COMPENSATE thus improves over the diffusion baseline by \textbf{16
		percentage points} and approximately halves the mean minimum
		position error on the same teacher-generated supervision. The gap between
		the two diffusion seeds (32\% vs. 66\%) is also nearly twice that of
		COMPENSATE (56\% vs. 74\%), suggesting that the per-environment teacher
		routing used during distillation yields more reproducible behavior than
		dataset-aggregated generative training, which must learn to disambiguate
		fault modes implicitly from a single mixed corpus.

	\subsection{Discussion}
	\label{subsec:results-discussion}

		The gap between the privileged teacher ensemble (90\%) and the fault-aware
		student (65\%) quantifies the cost of compressing the per-mode specialists
		into a single fault-conditioned network. Because the student receives the
		same observation as the teachers, including the fault indicator, the 25-point
		drop is attributable to distillation rather than to latent fault inference,
		and the fact that the student still solves roughly two-thirds of episodes
		confirms that a single network of this capacity can carry the bulk of the
		ensemble's behavioural repertoire. The diffusion
		baseline (which sees the same teacher data and the same fault conditioning
		but distills a single generative model rather than a routed student) is
		unable to close this gap to the same extent, recovering only about half of
		episodes and producing twice the residual position error, indicating that
		per-environment discriminative routing is more sample-efficient than
		generative aggregation under this supervision budget. Together these results
		provide preliminary evidence for the architectural decomposition underlying COMPENSATE:
		decoupling per-mode PPO training from a single unified, fault-conditioned
		deployable policy. These conclusions are drawn from the Reach task; whether
		they hold under the richer contact dynamics of Lift, and under
		degraded-stiffness and multi-joint faults, is the subject of the remaining
		evaluations summarized below and detailed in the appendix.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

	\textbf{Experimental scope.} The reported evaluation covers only the Reach task under per-joint
	permanent lockouts; degraded-stiffness faults, simultaneous multi-joint faults, and the Lift
	task are within the framework's modelled fault and task distribution but are
	not yet reported in the main text. Reach is the kinematic substrate of essentially every
	manipulation task we consider (Lift reduces to a sequence of Reach
	sub-goals composed with a gripper or contact event), so the per-joint specialists characterised
	here transfer directly to that task; nevertheless, a broader experimental sweep would have
	strengthened the empirical case, and we report preliminary Lift results in
	the appendix as a partial mitigation. The PPO-without-failures baseline defined in
	\S\ref{sec:result} is likewise pending. All numbers are sim-only: the zero-shot deployment on
	the Franka Research~3 and Franka Emika Panda arms is still in progress, so the mjlab
	cross-simulator probe currently functions as a necessary but not sufficient surrogate for
	hardware transfer.

	\textbf{Statistical resolution.} The student-vs-diffusion comparison rests on two training
	seeds per method. The within-method spread is large (COMPENSATE $56\%$ vs.\ $74\%$; diffusion
	$32\%$ vs.\ $66\%$) and the $16$-percentage-point headline is not yet supported by a sample
	size sufficient for a statistical-significance claim; a larger seed sweep is required before
	the gap can be treated as conclusive.

	\textbf{Oracle fault conditioning.} The student consumes a one-hot fault indicator
	$f\in\mathcal{F}$ supplied by an oracle. We have not characterised performance under noisy,
	delayed, or incorrect fault labels, nor coupled the controller to an upstream learned
	fault-detection module; consequently the reported numbers upper-bound what a deployed system
	would achieve once a realistic detector is inserted.

	\textbf{Ensemble scaling.} The privileged teacher ensemble grows linearly with $|\mathcal{F}|$.
	The fault set used here ($|\mathcal{F}|=8$: seven single-joint lockouts plus a healthy baseline) is small enough for one PPO
	specialist per mode to be tractable, but the combinatorial expansion to multi-joint and
	mixed-mode faults will outpace this scaling and motivate parameter-sharing teachers or
	hierarchical decompositions that we have not yet developed.

	\textbf{Goal distribution and embodiment.} Reach goals are rejection-sampled to remain
	reachable under the active lockout, which biases the evaluation toward kinematically feasible
	targets and may overstate success rates relative to an adversarial goal set. All experiments
	use a single $7$-DoF Franka model; transfer to other embodiments (mobile manipulators,
	bi-manual platforms, arms with different redundancy structure) is outside the present
	evaluation and is expected to require retraining the teacher ensemble from scratch.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

	We presented \textbf{COMPENSATE}, a teacher--student reinforcement learning
	framework for fault-tolerant control of robotic manipulators that compresses a
	per-mode PPO specialist ensemble into a single fault-conditioned deployable
	policy. The framework is composed of three interdependent components: a privileged teacher
	ensemble in which one PPO specialist is optimised per failure mode so that
	mode-specific compensations are not averaged away by a single multi-task agent,
	a fault-conditioned student that consumes an explicit fault indicator and is
	supervised by hard per-environment teacher routing so that the per-mode
	behaviour repertoire is absorbed by a single network, and a staged fault curriculum
	paired with zero-shot cross-simulator evaluation in mjlab that rejects policies
	exploiting simulator-specific contact integration prior to hardware deployment. Jointly, these
	components mitigate the multi-task interference that destabilises a single PPO agent under
	mixed-fault training while preserving a single deployable policy at inference.

	Our current evidence is restricted to the Reach task under permanent
	single-joint lockouts on a Franka Research~3 model. Within this regime the
	specialist teachers reach a mean success rate of 89.4\% and a mean minimum
	end-effector error of 0.038~m across the seven single-joint lockouts (with
	the healthy baseline at 94\%/0.037~m); the
	fault-aware student recovers 65.0\% of episodes at 0.051~m, outperforming a
	diffusion baseline trained on the same teacher data and fault conditioning by
	16 percentage points of success and roughly halving its residual position
	error. The 24-point gap between the privileged teachers and the student
	quantifies the cost of compressing the ensemble into a single fault-conditioned
	network and constitutes the most direct preliminary evidence for the architectural
	decomposition that separates per-mode optimisation from unified deployment.

	Beyond closing the experimental sweep noted in \S\ref{sec:limitations}, we plan to study
	generalisation to failure modes outside the teacher set, the sensitivity of the distillation
	recipe to teacher count and curriculum schedule, the substitution of the oracle fault
	indicator with an upstream learned detector, and the transfer of the
	privileged-teacher / fault-conditioned-student decomposition to other embodiments such as
	mobile manipulators and bi-manual platforms where the redundancy structure differs.
