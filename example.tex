\begin{abstract}
	Robotic manipulators operating in industrial and safety-critical environments are vulnerable to joint
	failures that can abruptly degrade performance, yet existing fault-tolerant strategies rely on accurate
	dynamic models or predefined recovery procedures that do not generalize across diverse failure modes.
	We present COMPENSATE, a deep reinforcement learning framework in which specialized teacher policies
	trained on individual failure configurations are distilled into a unified student policy that detects
	and adapts to joint failures in real time, without any explicit fault signal. By incorporating
	permanent lockouts, degraded stiffness, and simultaneous multi-joint faults into the training
	distribution, COMPENSATE learns compensation strategies that transfer to unseen failure types. We
	evaluate on reach and lift tasks in IsaacLab with cross-simulator validation in mjlab and zero-shot
	deployment on a Franka Research~3 arm. Against standard PPO, a diffusion policy baseline, and a
	fault-aware inverse kinematics controller, COMPENSATE achieves superior task completion rates and
	adaptation speed while maintaining smooth operational transitions under diverse joint failure conditions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

	Robotic manipulators are a cornerstone of modern industrial applications, performing repetitive,
	high-precision tasks with speed and reliability~\citep{ali2018vision, ghodsian2023mobile}. Recent
	advances in artificial intelligence~(AI) have expanded the capabilities of robotic systems, enabling
	them to learn, adapt, and make independent decisions~\citep{sun2021artificial, liu2021deep,
	li2024manipllm}. Yet despite these advances, a fundamental vulnerability persists: mechanical
	malfunctions (arising from joint wear, actuator fatigue, or unexpected environmental loading) can
	abruptly degrade performance or cause complete task failure~\citep{291826, sciavicco2012modelling}.
	As manipulators are deployed in safety-critical domains such as healthcare, manufacturing, and field
	robotics, the ability to maintain task performance through joint failures has become essential, while
	most deployed systems still rely on predefined, fault-unaware control
	policies~\citep{deloitte2025smart, allaboutai2025report}.

	Traditional fault management strategies address this vulnerability through hardware redundancy and
	fault diagnosis and isolation~(FDI). Redundancy-based approaches add actuators or parallel kinematic
	chains so that a single joint failure does not halt operation~\citep{291826, anand2012fault,
	mokhtari2022fault}, but incur substantial cost, weight, and design complexity. FDI techniques
	(spanning signal-processing anomaly detection~\citep{kimevaluation, ompusunggu2014winding}, rule-based
	expert systems~\citep{li2023open}, and model-based deviation monitors~\citep{shuai2022research,
	sabry2019fault}) can identify that a fault has occurred, yet they do not synthesize an adapted control
	policy in response; a separate, hand-crafted recovery layer is still required.

	Machine learning has begun to close this gap. Predictive maintenance and anomaly detection models can
	anticipate failures before they occur or flag deviations during operation~\citep{lian2022anomaly,
	eski2011fault}, and recent work demonstrates that learning directly from observed robot failures
	(including recovery in long-horizon manipulation tasks) is feasible~\citep{huang2025fail2progress}.
	Generative approaches such as counterfactual failure synthesis can further produce diverse fault
	rollouts from successful demonstrations, reaching 81.3\% correction accuracy~\citep{wang2025learning}.
	Nevertheless, these methods presuppose either a corpus of failure demonstrations or an explicit
	fault-detection signal as input; when a joint degrades silently mid-task and no labeled failure data
	are available, they cannot generate an online, adaptive control response.

	The robotics community has increasingly turned to reinforcement learning~(RL) to overcome this
	limitation. For legged locomotion, contrastive forward-prediction RL enables agents to detect joint
	damage from proprioceptive signals alone and immediately reconfigure gait without any explicit fault
	label~\citep{fu2025contrastive}. For visuomotor policies, latent-space RL can steer a pretrained
	diffusion policy toward successful outcomes with minimal additional
	samples~\citep{wagenmaker2025steering}, and sim-to-real transfer via massive parallel sampling with
	active domain exploration has demonstrated 42--63\% performance gains over strong
	baselines~\citep{sobanbabu2025sampling}. These results confirm that RL-based adaptation is a
	principled direction for fault-tolerant robotics, yet they only address locomotion. The specific
	challenge of reactive joint-failure compensation for \emph{robot manipulator} reaching tasks in real
	hardware deployments remains underexplored.

	To address this gap, we present \textbf{COMPENSATE}, a teacher-student reinforcement learning
	framework for fault-tolerant manipulator control. The framework rests on three coupled choices.
	\emph{(i)}~A \emph{privileged teacher ensemble} in which one PPO specialist is trained per
	failure mode, so that mode-specific compensations are not averaged away by a single multi-task
	agent. \emph{(ii)}~A \emph{fault-blind student} distilled by hard per-environment teacher
	routing, with no fault indicator in its observation, so that the active fault must be inferred
	from proprioception alone, matching what is available on hardware. \emph{(iii)}~A \emph{staged
	fault curriculum} for the teachers, paired with cross-simulator validation in mjlab that acts as
	an inexpensive sim-to-real probe filtering out engine-specific artifacts before any physical
	trial. We instantiate the framework on a Franka Research~3 model and report a preliminary
	evaluation on the Reach task under per-joint permanent lockouts; remaining failure regimes,
	tasks, and hardware deployments are noted as ongoing work.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

	\textbf{Classical fault-tolerant control.} Fault-tolerant control for robotic manipulators has long
	relied on model-based methods. Early strategies applied passive and active hardware redundancy so that
	joint failures would not halt operation, while fault diagnosis and isolation~(FDI) detected deviations
	from known system models~\citep{blanke, 291826, sciavicco2012modelling}. More recent designs embed
	backstepping or sliding-mode controllers that maintain tracking under actuator
	faults~\citep{anand2012fault, mokhtari2022fault, act11120353}. These methods are analytically rigorous
	but require accurate dynamics models and hand-crafted recovery rules, limiting their applicability to
	anticipated, well-characterized fault modes.

	\textbf{Learning-based fault detection and diagnosis.} Data-driven methods have substantially improved
	fault detection speed and accuracy. Neural networks, deep belief networks, and power-signature analysis
	can flag incipient anomalies before hard failures occur~\citep{eski2011fault, sabry2019fault,
	shuai2022research, raouf2022mechanical}. Inverse reinforcement learning has been applied to
	simultaneously detect anomalies and propose corrective actions in autonomous
	systems~\citep{lian2022anomaly}, and deep-learning diagnosis has been extended to multi-fault
	industrial robots~\citep{adam2023multiple}. Detection alone, however, does not yield an adapted
	control policy; a separate recovery layer is still required, and these methods are unreliable when
	failures evolve gradually with no labeled fault data available.

	\textbf{Reinforcement learning for fault-tolerant robots.} RL has emerged as a compelling framework
	for learning adaptive behaviors without explicit failure models. In legged locomotion, contrastive
	forward-prediction RL enables quadrupeds to detect joint damage from proprioceptive signals alone and
	immediately reconfigure gait without any fault label~\citep{fu2025contrastive}. Learning from observed
	failure trajectories has also shown promise for manipulation recovery~\citep{huang2025fail2progress,
	wang2025learning}, and RL has been compared favorably against model predictive control for
	fault-tolerant continuous control~\citep{ahmed2018comparison}. For manipulators specifically,
	\citet{pham2024adaptive} formulated joint-failure compensation as a partially observable Markov
	decision process and demonstrated high task success, but addressed only single-joint, simulation-only
	scenarios, leaving multi-fault and real-hardware settings open.

	\textbf{Teacher-student distillation and sim-to-real transfer.} Teacher-student RL has become the
	standard paradigm for deploying policies that must operate under partial observability at test time.
	A privileged teacher trained with full state access (e.g., exact fault vectors or terrain properties)
	is distilled into a student that operates on deployable observations alone~\citep{kumar2021rma}.
	Latent-space RL can further steer a pre-trained policy toward correct behavior with minimal additional
	samples~\citep{wagenmaker2025steering}, and active-exploration system identification during sim-to-real
	transfer yields 42--63\% performance gains over standard domain
	randomization~\citep{sobanbabu2025sampling}. COMPENSATE applies this paradigm to manipulator fault
	tolerance: fault-specialized teacher policies are distilled into a single student that compensates for
	joint failures zero-shot on physical hardware. The next section formalises this setting as a
	POMDP and develops the three components in turn.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

	The deployment setting we target has a sharp informational asymmetry: at training time we can
	choose what an agent observes, but at test time the arm provides only proprioception and the
	commanded action. Any method that relies on a fault label at deployment presupposes the very
	fault-detection module we wish to avoid. \textbf{COMPENSATE} therefore separates training-time
	privilege from deployment-time observability through three coupled components: a
	\emph{privileged teacher ensemble} (\S\ref{subsec:teachers}) that converges to mode-specific
	compensations rather than an averaged behaviour, a \emph{staged fault curriculum}
	(\S\ref{subsec:curriculum}) that keeps PPO stable as the fault set widens, and a
	\emph{fault-blind student} (\S\ref{subsec:distillation}) distilled by hard per-environment
	teacher routing so that compensation is inferred from kinematic history alone. We first
	formalise the setting.

	\subsection{Problem Formulation}
	\label{subsec:problem}

		We model joint-failure-resilient manipulation as a Partially Observable Markov
		Decision Process~(POMDP)
		$\langle\mathcal{S},\mathcal{O},\mathcal{A},T,R,\gamma\rangle$. The full state
		$s\in\mathcal{S}$ contains the joint positions, joint velocities, and the
		currently active failure configuration, but the policy never observes the
		failure component: $o\in\mathcal{O}$ is restricted to relative joint positions
		$q$, relative joint velocities $\dot q$, the previous action $a_{t-1}$, and the
		goal end-effector pose $g$ (3D position and unit quaternion). This deliberate
		partial observability is the methodological core of the work: it forces the
		agent to recover the latent failure state from observable kinematics, mirroring
		the information available on a real arm whose joint has silently degraded.

		Actions $a\in\mathcal{A}$ are relative joint-position increments scaled by
		$\alpha$. We consider a set $\mathcal{F}$ of failure configurations comprising
		single-joint permanent lockouts and a healthy baseline; degraded-stiffness and
		multi-joint variants are introduced in~\S\ref{subsec:curriculum}. Under lockout
		$f_i$, joint $i$ holds its last commanded position regardless of the network
		output, modelling a fault that the controller cannot bypass at the actuator
		level:
		\begin{equation}
		    q_t^{\mathrm{cmd}, i} =
		        \begin{cases}
		            q_{t-1}^{\mathrm{cmd}, i} + \alpha\,a_t^i & \text{joint } i \text{ healthy,}\\
		            q_{t-1}^{\mathrm{cmd}, i}                  & \text{joint } i \text{ locked.}
		        \end{cases}
		    \label{eq:action}
		\end{equation}
		Modelling the fault inside the action transition (rather than as an external
		disturbance on $T$) ensures that the gradient seen by the teacher reflects what
		the deployed arm will actually execute, eliminating a source of train--test
		drift that would otherwise have to be handled by domain randomization.

	\subsection{Privileged Teacher Ensemble}
	\label{subsec:teachers}

		Different joint failures induce qualitatively different optimal compensations:
		a locked shoulder must be circumvented by elbow and wrist redundancy, while a
		locked wrist requires a base-driven reorientation. A single PPO agent confronted
		with the full fault distribution receives gradient signals that pull these
		solutions toward an averaged, often infeasible, behavior. We therefore train
		one specialist~$\pi^c$ per configuration $c\in\mathcal{F}$ using
		PPO~\citep{schulman2017proximal}, treating each teacher as a privileged oracle
		that has implicit knowledge of its own fault through the environment it was
		exposed to during training.

		Each teacher optimizes a reward that combines end-effector tracking with motion
		regularization,
		\begin{equation}
		    r_t = w_\mathrm{pos}\,\|p_\mathrm{ee} - p_g\|_2
		          + w_\mathrm{fine}\,r_\mathrm{fine}(p_\mathrm{ee},\,p_g)
		          + w_\mathrm{ori}\,r_\mathrm{ori}(\mathbf{R}_\mathrm{ee},\,\mathbf{R}_g)
		          + w_\mathrm{reg}\,r_\mathrm{reg}(a_t,\,\dot{q}_t),
		    \label{eq:reward}
		\end{equation}
		where $r_\mathrm{fine}$ is a kernel-smoothed fine-positioning bonus and
		$r_\mathrm{reg}$ penalizes action rate and joint velocity. The fine-grained and
		regularization weights are annealed from zero so that the agent first learns to
		\emph{reach} under the active fault before being asked to do so \emph{smoothly};
		without this annealing we observed teachers collapsing to a still posture that
		trivially satisfies the regularizer. Goal poses are sampled by drawing random
		joint configurations, computing forward kinematics, and rejecting any pose
		outside the reachable workspace of the locked joint, so the reward signal is
		never dominated by physically unreachable targets, an effect that would
		otherwise bias the teacher toward unreachable-target avoidance rather than
		fault compensation.

	\subsection{Fault-Mode Curriculum}
	\label{subsec:curriculum}

		Even with one specialist per fault, exposing a teacher to all variants of its
		target mode from the first step (e.g., lockouts at different commanded
		positions, or jointly with stiffness degradation) destabilizes early training:
		the agent cannot disambiguate whether a failed reach is due to its own policy
		or to a fault it has not yet learned to model. We therefore use an incremental
		curriculum that begins with the teacher's target failure in isolation and
		expands the active fault set $\mathcal{C}_k\subseteq\mathcal{F}$ in stages over
		the course of training. This decouples skill acquisition from fault inference
		and is the only mechanism, aside from the privileged-teacher decomposition
		itself, that we found necessary to obtain stable PPO convergence under
		multi-joint and degraded-stiffness faults. The student does not use a
		curriculum: by the time distillation begins, all teachers are fixed, so
		uniform sampling over $\mathcal{F}$ poses no instability.

	\subsection{Fault-Blind Student Distillation}
	\label{subsec:distillation}

		The student~$\pi^S$ is the policy that will actually be deployed. To match
		hardware conditions, it receives the same proprioceptive observation as the
		teachers but \emph{no fault index}; the failure mode is a latent variable that
		the student must infer from kinematic history. This is what distinguishes
		COMPENSATE from prior teacher--student work that includes the privileged
		variable as a learned embedding~\citep{kumar2021rma}: we make the absence of
		the fault signal a deployment requirement, not a regularization trick, because
		any fault label at deployment time would presuppose the very FDI module our
		method is designed to replace.

		At each distillation reset, every parallel environment is independently
		assigned a configuration $c$ and routed to the corresponding teacher~$\pi^c$;
		that teacher provides the behavioral target for that environment for the
		entire episode. We use this \emph{hard routing} rather than averaging
		teacher logits because the teachers represent mode-specific compensations that
		are non-interpolable, so averaging would supervise the student with actions no
		single teacher would take. Given minibatches $\{(o_k,\,a_k^c)\}_{k=1}^{B}$, the
		student minimizes the per-action behavioral-cloning loss
		\begin{equation}
		    \mathcal{L}_\mathrm{BC} =
		        \frac{1}{B}\sum_{k=1}^{B}
		        \bigl\|\pi^S(o_k) - a_k^c\bigr\|_2^{2}.
		    \label{eq:bc}
		\end{equation}
		Because the student must reproduce all teachers from a fault-blind observation,
		convergence of~$\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{BC}$ is itself evidence that the
		compensation strategies are identifiable from proprioception, which in turn
		predicts that the student will generalize to fault modes that lie between
		those represented in $\mathcal{F}$. We now describe how these components are
		instantiated and evaluated.

\section{Experiments}
\subsection{Implementation Details}

		All policies are trained in \textbf{IsaacLab} on a Franka Research~3 (FR3)
		model, then evaluated through a deliberate three-stage pipeline (IsaacLab,
		mjlab, and physical hardware), each stage acting as a stricter filter on the
		preceding one. The policy observation is 32-dimensional: relative joint
		positions $q\in\mathbb{R}^{9}$, relative joint velocities
		$\dot q\in\mathbb{R}^{9}$, the previous action $a_{t-1}\in\mathbb{R}^{7}$, and
		a goal end-effector pose $g\in\mathbb{R}^{7}$. Actions are relative
		joint-position increments with scale $\alpha=0.03$~rad. The teacher set
		$\mathcal{F}$ contains $|\mathcal{F}|=8$ single-joint permanent lockout
		configurations across the actuated degrees of freedom of the FR3 model.
		The reach task resamples random
		end-effector goals every 6~s with success defined as $\le 0.04$~m position
		error and $\le 0.2$~rad orientation error; additive uniform observation noise
		of $\pm 0.01$~rad is injected on joint positions, velocities, and action
		feedback throughout training.

		\textbf{Networks and optimization.} Each teacher~$\pi^c$ is a two-layer MLP
		with hidden dimensions $[128,\,64]$ and ELU activations, trained with PPO for
		$3{,}500$ iterations using 24 rollout steps per parallel environment, an
		adaptive learning rate (target KL $=0.01$, initial $\eta=10^{-4}$), clip ratio
		$\epsilon=0.2$, and entropy coefficient $\beta=0.001$. Reward weights are
		$w_\mathrm{pos}=-2.0$ and $w_\mathrm{ori}=-1.0$, with the fine-grained and
		regularization weights annealed from zero over the first $5{,}000$ steps. The
		fault curriculum expands $\mathcal{C}_k$ by one additional mode every
		$\lfloor 1/|\mathcal{F}|\rfloor$ fraction of training. The student is a wider
		MLP $[128,\,128]$ with ELU activations, trained on the BC loss in
		Eq.~\ref{eq:bc} using Adam ($\eta=5\times 10^{-4}$), gradient accumulation
		over 15 steps, gradient clipping at norm~1.0, and 8 learning epochs per
		rollout.

		\textbf{Cross-simulator validation as a sim-to-real probe.} Before any
		hardware trial, the IsaacLab-trained student is evaluated zero-shot in
		\textbf{mjlab}~(MuJoCo~Warp) under a matched observation and action
		specification at a matched 30~Hz control rate (240~Hz physics, decimation~8).
		This step is methodological rather than logistical: it asks whether the
		learned compensation is a property of the manipulator dynamics or of the
		Isaac~Sim integrator. A policy that exploits engine-specific artifacts will
		pass IsaacLab evaluation but fail in mjlab, and would do the same on real
		hardware; mjlab thus serves as an inexpensive filter that catches this
		failure mode before risking a physical arm. Policies that transfer cleanly to
		mjlab are then deployed zero-shot on two physical manipulators, the Franka
		Research~3 and the Franka Emika Panda, completing the simulation-to-hardware
		pipeline.

\subsection{Tasks and Evaluation Protocol}

The full COMPENSATE evaluation protocol spans four manipulation tasks (Reach, Spline, Lift, Stack), two classes of simulated joint fault (permanent lockout and degraded stiffness via artificially increased joint stiffness), and a sim-to-hardware pipeline that culminates in zero-shot deployment on the FR3 and Panda arms. Reach is the precise target-acquisition task and is the focus of the present manuscript; Spline, Lift, and Stack extend the protocol to trajectory tracking, object lifting, and sequential placement, and are evaluated in the same way once the corresponding teacher specialists have been trained. The primary metric is task completion rate, with completion time and (for Spline) a trajectory-tracking error reported as secondary metrics. Permanent lockouts constrain the affected joint to its last commanded position with zero velocity; degraded-stiffness faults preserve partial operability but inject diminished accuracy and compliance. Reported results below cover the Reach task under per-joint permanent lockouts; the remaining task-by-fault cells are left to subsequent reports.

\subsection{Baselines}

To benchmark the proposed method, we compare it against three baselines: a standard PPO policy trained without joint failures, a diffusion policy, and an inverse kinematics (IK) controller. The diffusion baseline is trained by rolling out the teacher specialists across all modelled joint-failure scenarios, aggregating the resulting transitions into a single dataset, and distilling that dataset into a diffusion policy; this provides a generative learning baseline that sees the same supervisory signal as COMPENSATE but lacks the per-environment teacher routing. The IK baseline is given an unfair advantage: the index and frozen position of the failed joint are passed directly to the solver, which then reconfigures the manipulator over the remaining degrees of freedom. This baseline represents an oracle classical-control variant in which fault detection is assumed solved, and serves as an upper-bound reference for what a fault-aware analytical controller can achieve.

\section{Experimental Results}
\label{sec:result}

	On the Franka Research~3 Reach task under per-joint permanent lockouts, the privileged
	specialist teachers attain a mean success rate of \textbf{90.0\%} at \textbf{0.038~m} mean
	end-effector error, and the fault-blind COMPENSATE student retains \textbf{65.0\%} success at
	\textbf{0.051~m}, outperforming a diffusion policy trained on the same teacher-generated data
	by \textbf{16 percentage points} of success and approximately halving its residual position
	error. The remainder of this section unpacks this headline through three questions, all
	evaluated on the \textbf{Reach} task. \emph{(Q1)} Can a specialist teacher recover end-effector
	reaching under a permanent single-joint lockout, and how does difficulty vary
	with the locked joint? \emph{(Q2)} Does the fault-blind student preserve the
	teachers' compensation strategies when the fault indicator is withheld?
	\emph{(Q3)} How does the student compare to a generative learning baseline
	trained on the same teacher data? Unless stated otherwise, all numbers are
	computed from 50 Reach episodes per (method, condition) cell using the
	success criterion of \S\ref{subsec:problem} ($\le 0.04$~m position error
	within the 6~s episode budget); we also report the minimum end-effector
	position error attained within each episode (mean, standard deviation, and
	best across the 50 trials) as a continuous proxy for compensation quality.
	Results on the Spline, Lift, and Stack tasks, on degraded-stiffness and
	multi-joint faults, and on the PPO-without-failures and IK baselines, follow
	the same protocol and are reported in the appendix and in the
	hardware-deployment section.

	\subsection{Discussion}
	\label{subsec:results-discussion}

		The gap between the privileged teacher ensemble (90\%) and the fault-blind
		student (65\%) quantifies the cost of removing the fault indicator at
		deployment. Because the student receives the same proprioceptive
		observation as the teachers, the 25-point drop is attributable entirely to
		latent fault inference, and the fact that the student still solves roughly
		two-thirds of episodes confirms that the active failure mode is, in
		principle, recoverable from kinematic dynamics alone. The diffusion
		baseline (which sees the same teacher data but distils a single generative
		model rather than a routed student) is unable to close this inference gap
		to the same extent, recovering only about half of episodes and producing
		twice the residual position error. Together these results provide
		preliminary support for the central design choice of COMPENSATE: separating
		the policy capacity that is privileged at training time from the
		observation budget that must be respected at deployment time. These
		conclusions are drawn from the Reach task; whether they hold under the
		richer contact dynamics of Lift and Stack, and under degraded-stiffness
		and multi-joint faults, is the subject of the remaining evaluations
		summarized below and detailed in the appendix.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

	We presented \textbf{COMPENSATE}, a teacher--student reinforcement learning
	framework for fault-tolerant control of robotic manipulators that does not
	rely on an explicit fault-detection signal at deployment. The framework
	rests on three coupled choices: a privileged teacher ensemble in which one
	PPO specialist is trained per failure mode so that mode-specific
	compensations are not averaged away, a fault-blind student distilled by
	hard per-environment teacher routing so that the active fault must be
	inferred from proprioception alone, and a staged fault curriculum paired
	with cross-simulator validation in mjlab that filters out engine-specific
	artifacts before any hardware trial. Together these choices target the
	silent-failure regime that classical FDI pipelines and prior teacher-with-
	privileged-embedding methods do not address.

	Our current evidence is restricted to the Reach task under permanent
	single-joint lockouts on a Franka Research~3 model. Within this regime the
	specialist teachers reach a mean success rate of 90.0\% and a mean minimum
	end-effector error of 0.038~m across the eight failure configurations; the
	fault-blind student recovers 65.0\% of episodes at 0.051~m, outperforming a
	diffusion baseline trained on the same teacher data by 16 percentage points
	of success and roughly halving its residual position error. The 25-point
	gap between the privileged teachers and the fault-blind student quantifies
	the cost of withholding the fault indicator, and is the most direct
	preliminary support we have for the central design choice of decoupling
	training-time privilege from deployment-time observability.

	Several evaluations are still in progress. We have not yet reported results
	on degraded-stiffness or simultaneous multi-joint faults, on the Spline,
	Lift, and Stack tasks, or against the PPO-without-failures and IK
	baselines, and the zero-shot hardware deployment on the Franka Research~3
	and Franka Emika Panda arms remains to be carried out. Beyond completing
	this experimental sweep, we plan to study generalisation to failure modes
	outside the teacher set, the sensitivity of the distillation recipe to
	teacher count and curriculum schedule, and the transfer of the privileged-
	teacher / fault-blind-student decomposition to other embodiments such as
	mobile manipulators and bi-manual platforms where the redundancy structure
	differs.
